The Canonical Trick Play & Its Punishment

You can download all of the SGFs used in this post here. I use Sabaki as my editor, which features some extra bling on the game tree. So, in order to extract the most from my SGFs, I advise you to use it too. Don't forget to donate to the project too!

This wasn't supposed to be that long. I wanted it to be a simple post with a short English version of a tiny video a I was going to post in Portuguese for . However, before creating the short post I decided to ask Reddit (r/baduk) if anyone had anything to say about the this typical trick-punishment pattern (see Diagram 2). It turns out Uberdude85 knew of something quite substantial that I didn't: the typical punishment is suboptimal/wrong. His rebuttal has been corroborated by a fair number of pros and strong amateurs recently as well.

I'm 1 dan on most Go servers, so I do have some authority, if you care about these things.

In the next section, I'll expose the digested version of all the sequences, but all of them are in the SGF accompanying this post. You can also dive deep into the rabbit hole, if you wish, by going through all of the collected references in the last section.

Andrew Simons also points out that the more correct punishment was known even before the AI era. AIs nowadays even go as far as to rank the descent as worse than the hane itself.

Interestingly enough, some pros have played this move, and the games are searchable through Waltheri's Go Pattern Search. Nevertheless, more surprisingly, even Shusaku has faced this pattern in one of his games; and, even more astonishing, is that he seems to subscribe to the supposedly more correct punishment.

The trick play in question comes from one of the first josekis most people learn, shown in Diagram 1.

Interestingly enough, what I'm calling a trick play is actually called a mistake by most — slightly more experienced — players, the hane at A, in Diagram 2.

There are many options on how to react for White, mostly ranging from B to E; tenuking is usually not one of them, since taking the corner or having the either B or E is quite big.

B would evolve into a typical 3-3 invasion, as seen in Diagram 3. This would clearly be inferior to the joseki shown previously, in Diagram 1 — it's worth mentioning that this type of loss is not the end of the world, most mistakes are actually made during the middle game anyway.

E seems to be not so unlikely due to White's stone on the left, but Black's shape after C is very good. White will probably crawl on the second line in order to live, as in Diagram 4.

All that's left for White now is, then, to either C or D, in Diagram 2.

C is what basically everyone learns as a DDK or high SDK, despite it being quite the weird shape. It does work, but the question is: is it the best White can do? Or: is this all even a trick-punish ordeal or a (more or less) new joseki? Those are assessments you will have to decide for yourself, but I do believe most "reasonable" sequences are way more even than most players want to acknowledge.

Let's dive a bit deeper on C first.

The reason C has become such a staple of a trick play is that then E in Diagram 2 becomes fairly easy to do, and White successfully captures a big corner, as seen in Diagram 5.

There are many ways of showing that Black's situation becomes quite hopeless after Q18, and I'm not going to show all of them, but Diagram 6 and Diagram 7 should illustrate two other important ways of finishing the position for White.

The thing is... the descent as a play has induced a generation of players to fall for two mistakes making a right, in my opinion. The cut doesn't seem to work, and the descent looks even weirder. But, when Black realizes its cut was a mistake, the sequence comes back into something more grounded in reality. A simple connection puts Black in a position where it has quite the thick wall, as seen in Diagram 8.

This wall is very thick, it's even thicker than most enclosure josekis. Even though A doesn't fully connect to the stone on the left, it's not as if other enclosure josekis didn't have their problems either. The banal enclosure joseki at the bottom of Diagram 9 features glaring weaknesses around B and C, for instance. In the new pattern, even if Black doesn't fully enclose — which is seen in the next section, in a pro game, by the way —, Black still has D available, to form a base for the stone at the top.

Another Black could enclose White, seen in Diagram 10, is what most other players have suggested as the main sequence for disproving the descent as the punishment to the trick play.

However, I think the cleanest example to debunk the descent is actually Diagram 11, which is also what's shown on Sensei's Library. Black gets a very thick and clean shape from the tiger's mouth.

Now, I hope we can move on to move D, the hane, from Diagram 2.

The most important sequence which differentiates the descent from the hane is the final shape after Black's connection. It should be clear to most dan players that the shape in Diagram 12 is much stronger than White connecting at C — I'm sorry to weaker players, but this is a discussion you'll have elsewhere.

Black can try to resist, but the end result won't look nice, as seen in Diagram 13, the A stone is severely damaged, Black has poor shape and hasn't even captured the White stones to the right.

In terms of resistance, the best Black can hope for is using his stones to force White's enclosure — which is seen in 2 pro games in the next section —, as in Diagram 14. Black can and should probably fix its wall with a tiger's mouth, to protect B and C at the same time, and White will have sente to diminish the effect of the wall. White's corner is bigger than usual, and so is Black's thickness; the final result is much more even than expected in my opinion, even though the wall is in an unexpected direction, given the initial position.

This weird direction of play from Black isn't really all that weird after witnessing some recent AI josekis, though, is it?

Black could offer a lot of resistance if there's a little bit of reinforcement in the area. Diagrams 15 shows how a single stone can change the outcome completely.

If Black has a stone to the side, as in Diagram 15, it is wiser to revert back to a 3-3 invasion, like Diagram 16. In fact, it's likely the optimal choice, since it makes Black's original stone on the side way less efficient than desired. Curiously, this is what Tono Hiroaki 9p played against Cho Chikun 9p in 1991, mentioned in the next section.

The discussion on the hane actually doesn't end optimally with Diagram 14. Most references point the hane plus clamp as the most likely optimal play for both sides, as in Diagram 17. A for B is aji-keshi, a bad exchange for Black. Pros seem to think this is enough of a punishment, but I'll let you decide: isn't this discussion the epitome of the road being more important than the destination: all this to end up with a "minimal" aji-keshi solution?!

This is a cut I'm analyzing on my own, using Leela as a crutch and confirmation tool. I think the overall results make sense, but, as always, I'm open to feedback.

One final variation that bugged me out quite a bit is the cut after a kosumi in the descent variations, which is shown below, in Diagram 18.

Black could have reverted the course to more normal variations by plaing 14 at 15, but, after White 13, Leela also sees Black 14 as the only option.

Black is still worse here. My first intuition was to give Black the corner while squashing from the outside, and that's exactly what Leela thought as optimal, which is shown in Diagram 19.

The closest I got to finding a successful professional use of this trick play is a game between Kang Dongyun and Lee Jaeung, back in 2007, when they were both 5p. Kang Dongyun 9p uses this pattern to both enclose the corner and switch to developing the left side, something which wouldn't have been possible with the standard joseki in this case.
